I spent nearly a year finding my Malamute. Not because puppies were scarce — there were plenty. But because every time I visited a listing or contacted a kennel, something felt off. In the end I found the right one, and the difference was evident from the first phone call.

Finding a good breeder takes time and patience. It is absolutely worth it.

## Where to start

In most countries, national kennel clubs maintain registers of breeders affiliated with their breed clubs. In the UK, the Kennel Club; in the US, the American Kennel Club (AKC) and the Alaskan Malamute Club of America (AMCA); in Europe, national FCI-affiliated clubs. Breed club members have typically agreed to a code of ethics that includes health testing.

This isn't a guarantee of quality — registration certifies pedigree, not health or temperament — but it's a useful first filter.

Breed-specific clubs often have breeder referral lists, which are worth checking. The people on these lists have usually been vetted by the club.

## Questions to ask before you visit

A good breeder will answer these readily. Vague or evasive answers are information.

**About health testing:**
- Have both parents been tested for hip and elbow dysplasia? What are their scores? (OFA, BVA, or equivalent)
- Have they been tested for hereditary eye conditions (CAER)?
- Is there any history of hypothyroidism, polyneuropathy or other conditions in the lines?

**About the puppy:**
- At what age will the puppy be released? (Never before 8 weeks — 10-12 is better)
- What vaccinations has it received? Is it microchipped?
- Has it been wormed?
- How has socialisation been managed?

**About the breeding programme:**
- How many litters does the kennel produce per year?
- Do puppies live in the house or in outdoor kennels?
- Can I meet both parents? (At minimum, the mother must be present)
- Do you provide a written contract?
- Are you available for questions and support after the sale?

## What to observe during your visit

Never buy a puppy you haven't visited in person. Anyone who proposes distance delivery or sends only photos is an immediate red flag.

**The puppies:**
- Active, curious, engaged — not lethargic or withdrawn
- Clean eyes with no discharge
- Clean coat, no unpleasant smell
- No bloated belly (a sign of worm burden)
- Able to interact with people and littermates without extreme fear or aggression

**The environment:**
- Clean — not sterile, because puppies need a stimulating environment, but clean
- Adult dogs looking healthy and well-cared for
- Mother available and able to be seen with the puppies

**The breeder:**
- Asks you questions — about your home, your lifestyle, your experience with large dogs, whether you have a garden
- Isn't in a hurry to make the sale
- Knows the breed deeply and answers questions with real specifics
- Is willing to discourage you if your situation isn't right for a Malamute

## Red flags you should never ignore

**Price that seems too low** — A puppy with proper health testing, registration and early care costs money. If the price seems too good to be true, it usually is.

**Puppies always available** — A responsible breeding programme produces few litters per year. Always-available puppies suggest volume production, not careful breeding.

**No health testing on parents** — Non-negotiable. Hip dysplasia is too prevalent in the breed to overlook this.

**Release before 8 weeks** — Illegal in many countries and genuinely harmful to the puppy's development. Early separation causes real behavioural problems.

**Deposit required before a visit** — A classic puppy mill or scam pattern.

**No support offered after sale** — A good breeder remains a resource for the dog's entire life.

## Waiting lists are normal and fine

The best kennels have waiting lists. This is a feature, not a bug. Put your name down, use the waiting time to prepare properly, and trust the process. A year's wait for the right puppy is an investment in fifteen years of shared life. It's worth it.
